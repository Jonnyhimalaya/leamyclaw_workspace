# New Company AI Assessment Protocol
**Purpose:** A repeatable method for the first visit / first discovery session with a new company.

---

## Goal of the First Visit
Do **not** try to sell a full AI transformation in one conversation.

The goal is to understand:
1. how the company actually operates
2. where work gets stuck, delayed, duplicated, or forgotten
3. what tools already exist
4. where AI could create leverage safely
5. what the first high-value pilot should be

The first visit is an **operational diagnosis**, not just a sales pitch.

---

# Phase 1 — Pre-Visit Preparation
Before meeting the company:

## 1. Basic External Recon
Gather public context:
- website
- Google profile / reviews
- social media
- booking/contact flows
- public tech clues (WordPress, Shopify, booking engine, etc.)
- public complaints / praise

## 2. Business Snapshot
Write down:
- sector
- size
- likely operational model
- key customer journeys
- obvious pain points from the outside

## 3. Bring a discovery framework
Go in with a structured lens:
- workflows
- people
- tools
- data
- bottlenecks
- approvals
- customer communication

---

# Phase 2 — First Visit Discovery

## A. Understand the Business Model
Ask:
- How does the business make money?
- What are the main revenue streams?
- What does success look like weekly / monthly?
- What is most operationally stressful?
- What is currently blocking growth?

### Output
A clear picture of:
- money flow
- service delivery
- operational pressure points

---

## B. Map the Core Workflows
You want to uncover how work actually moves.

### Ask them to walk you through:
1. new enquiry / lead comes in
2. booking / sale happens
3. delivery / fulfilment happens
4. follow-up happens
5. issues / exceptions happen
6. reporting / management happens

### Questions
- What happens first?
- Then what?
- Who does that?
- In what tool?
- What usually goes wrong?
- What gets delayed?
- What depends on one person remembering?

### Output
A simple workflow map for each major business process.

---

## C. Identify the Friction Points
This is the heart of the assessment.

Look for:
- repetitive admin
- copy/paste work
- inbox chaos
- slow follow-up
- forgotten tasks
- handoff failures
- manual reporting
- missed opportunities
- staff doing low-value coordination
- customer response lag

### Golden question
**"What are the things you know should happen every day/week, but often don't?"**

That usually reveals the best AI opportunities.

---

## D. Audit the Tech Stack
Understand the real systems.

### Make a list of:
- CRM
- PMS / booking system / POS / ERP
- CMS / website platform
- email system
- spreadsheets
- WhatsApp / Telegram / phone
- scheduling/calendar tools
- analytics tools
- reporting tools
- payment systems
- storage / docs

### For each tool ask:
- Is this the source of truth?
- Does it have an API?
- Is it actively used or half-abandoned?
- Who owns it?
- Who has logins?
- Is it reliable?
- Is it clean or messy?

### Output
A tech map with:
- source-of-truth systems
- integration possibilities
- brittle areas
- manual-only areas

---

## E. Understand Human Roles and Approval Paths
AI cannot be designed well until you understand authority.

### Ask:
- Who makes decisions?
- Who approves what?
- Who replies to customers?
- Who can change pricing?
- Who can issue refunds?
- Who handles complaints?
- Who knows the workarounds when systems break?

### Output
A real org / decision map, not just a formal org chart.

---

## F. Assess Data Quality
AI is only as good as the data / process around it.

### Check:
- Are records complete?
- Are notes stored consistently?
- Are naming conventions chaotic?
- Are there duplicate systems?
- Is reporting trusted?
- Is important information trapped in one person's head?

### Output
A quality rating for the company's operational data.

---

# Phase 3 — Opportunity Identification
Once you understand workflows + tools + friction, score opportunities.

## The 5 Opportunity Filters
For each candidate AI workflow, score:

### 1. Frequency
How often does this happen?
- daily
- weekly
- occasionally

### 2. Pain
How annoying / costly is it?
- minor
- meaningful
- severe

### 3. Structure
Is the workflow structured enough for AI?
- highly structured = strong candidate
- chaotic / edge-case heavy = weaker candidate initially

### 4. Risk
What happens if AI gets it wrong?
- low risk = good pilot candidate
- high risk = require stronger controls

### 5. Leverage
Would this meaningfully save time, money, or lost opportunities?

---

# Phase 4 — Categorise AI Suitability
Every workflow should fall into one of 4 categories.

## Category 1 — Ideal for AI now
Characteristics:
- repetitive
- structured
- high volume
- low-to-medium risk
- easy to verify

Examples:
- review monitoring
- FAQ replies
- inbox triage
- daily briefings
- follow-up reminders
- content drafting

## Category 2 — AI-assisted only
Characteristics:
- useful, but needs human approval

Examples:
- pricing changes
- sensitive customer replies
- campaign edits
- contract/document summaries
- operational recommendations

## Category 3 — Observe first
Characteristics:
- valuable, but the workflow is messy or politically sensitive

Examples:
- staff coordination
- maintenance routing
- finance summaries
- internal escalations

## Category 4 — Avoid for now
Characteristics:
- high risk
- poor data
- unclear authority
- too much operational ambiguity

Examples:
- payments
- payroll
- legal commitments
- anything where a bad write creates serious liability

---

# Phase 5 — Recommend the First Pilot
The first pilot should be:
- visible
- useful
- low risk
- quick to prove value
- tied to a real pain point

## Strong pilot examples
- daily operational briefing
- review monitor + draft response system
- enquiry triage + follow-up
- content engine
- booking / lead follow-up assistant
- drip release / membership access workflow

## Weak pilot examples
- full autonomous operations
- deep ERP replacement
- finance automation first
- broad multi-agent sprawl before proving value

---

# Phase 6 — Design Recommendation Output
After the visit, produce a short structured document:

## 1. Business summary
- what they do
- how they make money
- key pressure points

## 2. Workflow map
- major workflows
- actors
- tools
- bottlenecks

## 3. Tech stack map
- systems in use
- source-of-truth tools
- API/browser/manual possibilities

## 4. AI opportunity shortlist
For each opportunity:
- problem
- proposed agent/workflow
- expected value
- risk level
- autonomy level

## 5. Recommended pilot
- what to build first
- why first
- expected ROI / time saved
- what approval model to use

## 6. Longer-term roadmap
- what could come later once trust is earned

# Field Heuristics — What to Look For

## Signs the company is a good AI client
- small team wearing too many hats
- lots of recurring admin
- owner/manager overloaded
- existing systems already in place
- clear pain points
- open to phased rollout
- values follow-up and consistency

## Signs to be cautious
- no one owns operations
- chaotic data everywhere
- unrealistic expectations ("replace staff next month")
- no decision maker involved
- too much sensitivity around access / systems
- no clear high-value use case

---

# Questions That Usually Unlock the Best Insights
Use these often.

- What gets dropped when things get busy?
- What do you repeat every day/week that feels like a waste of human time?
- What only works because one person remembers everything?
- What should happen consistently but doesn't?
- Where do customers wait too long for an answer?
- What reports do you wish existed automatically?
- What changes would make the biggest difference in the next 30 days?

---

- obvious quick wins

# Summary
The first company visit should answer:
1. **Where is the friction?**
2. **How does work actually move?**
3. **What tools run the business?**
4. **What is safe for AI to touch?**
5. **What is the best first pilot?**

The correct output is not:
"Here are 20 AI ideas."

The correct output is:
"Here is the business workflow map, here are the 3 best leverage points, and here is the first agentic system we should build."
